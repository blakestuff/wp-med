Villagers: The Reeve

The Reeve

The chief village appointed official was the reeve. Usually a shrewd and prosperous villein (holding a full virgate), the reeve was responsible for the day to day management of all sowing, ploughing and reaping on the lord’s own farmland. The position was quite an undertaking, the lords demesne could be as much as ten times the land a the reeve was used to managing. Couple that with supervising the work of 50+ fellow farmers and their wives and you can imagine why some men paid to avoid the responsibility.

The reeve’s chief function was that of estate accountant. Often illiterate but with an excellent memory the reeve made tallies and recorded figures detailing all the agricultural comings and goings over the course of the year. With the aid of a clerk a set of end of year accounts could be prepared for the lord’s steward covering the sale of produce, collection of monies, payment of accounts along with a tally of stock held; basically an early form of double entry book keeping.

His other duties often included:

  • Making sure that the villagers who owed labour service rose promptly and reported for work.
  • Seeing that the ploughs were yoked early and that the land was properly ploughed and sow.
  • The penning and folding of the lord’s livestock.
  • Making sure that sufficient forage (livestock fodder and fire wood) was saved for winter.
  • Chairing the village court and adjudicating on disputes.

Every year a new reeve would be appointed by the villagers at Michaelmas (Sep 29), the beginning of the agricultural year. However a fair reeve who carried out his duties efficiently who was trusted by the lord and the peasants alike was likely to stay in office more or less permanently.

Compensation took form of an exemption from villein obligations such as week-work and boon-work at harvest time. Other incentives were the right to graze his animals on the lords pasture and possibly taking his meals at the manor house table. Lastly, given so much responsibility it would have been relatively easy for the reeve to compensate himself by cheating the lord out a few bushels of wheat and a piglet or two.

Note: The bailiff and reeve seem to have very similar responsibilities. The key difference appears to be that the bailiff is appointed by the lord and the reeve is appointed or voted by the villagers.

Bailiff was the term used by the Normans for what the Saxons had called a reeve.
