Village

Introduction

The village was the backbone of the medieval economy. It’s inhabitants were mostly peasant farmers who’s hard work fed the village as well as providing a surplus for the clergy and their lord. This surplus in turn fed the towns and cities, freeing up merchants and entrepreneurs to create, buy and sell their goods far and wide.

In the interest of understanding how a medieval village functioned I’m going to use available data to recreate an idealised middle age feudal community. The notional village I intend to explore will date from around the early to mid 1300’s, comprise approximately 250 inhabitants and cover an area of roughly 2 square miles.

I’ll use this ‘model’ settlement to gain a better understanding of how the land was divided between the lord, the clergy and the tenant farmers. I want to find out who in the village had surplus goods and could make a profit and who lived on or even below the breadline.

I’m aiming to understand just how much extra food each community could produce to get an idea of how many villages it would take to support a market town. This data in turn can be extrapolated to get an idea of how cities were fed.

Background

Where there is a good water source coupled with a stretch of farmable land you will probably find a medieval hamlet or village thereabouts.

A typical village was a collection of houses surrounding a green or stretched out along a thoroughfare usually with a stone church and a mill. Most communities had a lord and there was a social hierarchy of free peasants and bondsmen even before the Norman’s invasion in the Eleventh Century. 

After the conquest England had a new master, it’s governance was handed down to French nobility in return for fealty and pledges of allegiance in times of war. Each town, village, hamlet and stead was audited, it’s inhabitants and livestock documented and a landholder appointed to raise tax and govern.

With these new land ownership came a more formalised social and political structure called feudalism whereby all people were now considered tenants and required to give fealty, labour and tax in exchange for military protection.

By the 1300’s conquest was a distant memory and England’s population was on the rise. It’s cities were flourishing with increased overseas trade but the feudal system was still in effect and for the majority (95% of the population) village life remained a tough and unrelenting existence.

Statistics

I’m borrowing the figures from a scaled down version of the village of Elton as detailed in Frances and Joseph Gies – ‘Life in a medieval village’. Elton is a large village by medieval standards with upwards of 600 inhabitants. I’ve scaled the population of the settlement back to 250 people as smaller village communities of between 100 – 300 inhabitants were more common during the 1300’s.

I’m going to slightly simplify the numbers so that I can easily half or even quarter them should I need a data set for a smaller village in the future. I’m aiming to collate the data I find into with the intent of visualising medieval life in the form of tables, maps and illustrations.

Maps

I needed to visualise the scale of the town and surrounding farmland so after a few false starts I embarked on the lengthy process of digitally drawing a map. It’s hopefully not a million miles from being to scale, I used a number of different reference points but accurate medieval village plans to base this on eluded me.

I’ve taken liberties and imagined the size of various features like the two flooded mill ponds, the orchard and pasture land surrounding the village itself. I’ve also conveniently surrounded the village on all sides with either woodland or marsh which would have limited its expansion.

Scale wise the whole area of the map is roughly 3.25 square miles with the village occupying approximately 2 square miles. The four roads north, south, east and west radiating from the centre of the village are all about 0.8 miles in length to the edges of the farmland.

The houses them selves varied in size from 10-15 feet wide and 20-40 feet long. Where wealth permitted houses would be wider, longer and grander with wings and multiple storeys but these were rare. The largest dwellings in the village are those of the merchant landowner’s house, the two mills, the parish tithe barns, the manor obviously and the church.

The people

Farmers and tradesmen

With the village farmland neatly apportioned between the bonded tenants and the freeholders I can begin to explore the time and effort that each inhabitant invested into feeding and maintaining the settlement.

The wealthier villein farmers owed a portion of their weekly toil to the manor so had to divide their time between working their own land and farming the lord’s demesne. Those with less land and feudal obligation could split their time between their fields and those of a wealthier neighbour for a fee. Others with only small farms might earn their crust in the pay of the manor or the clergy ploughing and sowing the lord’s or the church’s fields.

Those without land, the millers, bakers, smiths, wrights and other tradesmen earned a living supplying the necessary services, grinding grain, baking bread, fixing ploughs and repairing broken and worn tools. Poorer cottagers scraped a living tending animals, carting goods, serving the better off and labouring at the hundred and one tasks that kept the village afloat.

As an exercise in understanding just who did what within the village I’ll focus on each group of landholders and tradesmen and explore their lives in detail.

Notes: The office of parish priest came with an annual stipend from the manor but the main source of income for the parish clergy came from the the annual tithe of village produce and livestock. In addition enumerate small gifts and collections would be made over the course of the years as parishioners were baptised, married, given the last rites and attended mass.

The church also owned nearly 50 acres of farm land which turned a modest profit after the labour costs of sowing, ploughing and harvesting were deducted. Surplus produce along with the 10% village tithe would be sold at the local market with proceeds going to regional religious institutions and monastic organisations.

The parish priest could also be known as a rector, parson or vicar.

Notes: With the exception of the manorial lord and his bailiff the clothier is probably the most important and certainly the wealthiest landowner in the village. His family have been in the clothing trade for a number of generations and he’s established mercantile opportunities in many of the neighbouring market towns.

With the help of his extended family (his brother and family live next door and the fulling mill tenant is an in-law) the clothier manages over 100 acres of farmland, adjoining pastures, cottages with tenants and a complex domestic clothing business involving over 30 villagers.

Cloth production requires a plentiful supply of wool and for this reason the clothier has a large herd of sheep and shepherds in his employ to tend and when it is time shear them of their valuable fleeces. The wool is then carded or combed before being spun into yarn by an army of part time workers. Weavers, dyers and fullers turn the yarn into lengths of the cloth ready for market. A fulling mill was built in the village as a business venture between the previous lord and the clothier’s grandfather.

  • Villein / freeholder: 4 families (16 people) / 8 families (32 people)
  • Occupation(s): Farming (crops and livestock).
  • Income (daily / yearly): 1d / 240d
  • Outgoings (yearly): Food 120d / Rent 20d / Fines + other payments 20d
  • Status and office: Moderate – Some amongst the half virgaters would have held office within the village. juror, ale-taster or head of tithings.
  • Manorial duties: Weekly, probably little more than a single day of unpaid work. Annually, a few weeks helping to gather the lord’s harvest.
  • Notes: Moderate – Villagers with half a virgate virgate of land were likely to be amongst the most affluent within the community. In a good year farming 8 acres (4 of the 12 acres would be set aside as fallow each year) of land should have produced enough food to feed the virgate holders family with a little surplus set aside to sell at market.

“At the top of the heap were the large landholders, practically lords in and of themselves. They might own a hide of land (roughly 120 acres), portions of which they would in turn rent out to other peasants. Almost all of these wealthy landholders were free, and lived on the cusp of gentrification. They might even aspire to marry into the local aristocracy. Not all villages had such individuals (they would comprise less than 5% of the village population), and where they existed they more closely resembled secondary manorial holdings in their own right.

Below these families were those of the Half-Virgaters (holding around 15 to 20 acres); about a quarter of the village populace fell into this category. Half-Virgaters could be expected to turn a very modest profit in a good year, and break even in a bad one. As with full Virgaters, about one-in-five of the Half-Virgaters were free, but even those who weren’t free owed only half the labor obligations, just as they owned half the amount of land. Half-Virgaters could rarely save enough to hope to improve their conditions unless they practiced a trade on the side, or were very industrious with their free time on the labor market.

Another step down the scale were the Quarter Virgaters, with 8-12 acres of land (the name being only an approximation). Quarter-Virgaters made up a relatively small class of about 10% of the population. They would just break even in a good year, and would often have to supplement their income with labor. A little over half of these peasants were of free status, though almost all would willingly take on villein status if it meant more land. Quarter-Virgaters only owed a half-day every week, but would also be expected to work the lord’s harvest. Their rents were often higher per acre than those of the Half-Virgater or Virgater.

Finally, at the bottom of the village social scale, were the Smallholders, each owning less than 5 acres and often no land aside from a small garden behind a house. Smallholders comprised 40% of the village population, representing its largest class of resident. Smallholders owed very minimal labor services (two-thirds were free), but would have to spend most of their time working for others to make ends meet. Many of these Smallholders were also craftsmen: smiths, plowmen, carpenters, and other skilled tradesmen of the village. As such, they might be respected members of the community, but in an agrarian economy, land was the true signifier of wealth.”

I better explain my interpretation of a virgate into acreage as there is much conjecture as to how much farmland this actually equated to. I’m approximating a virgate to be equal to roughly 30 acres as I’ve read it could have been anywhere between 20 and 40 acres depending on the date, country and circumstances. I’m working to a half virgate of 15 acres, a quarter of 8 acres (as 7.5 makes for difficult maths) and an eighth of 4 acres (again for simplicities sake).

Work

I’m going to make the assumption that a peasant farmer could effectively farm 4 acres of land in a day. This is a rough estimate based on the fact that often stated area of farmland given to villein by a landowning lord was a virgate (30 acres), so they must have been able to work that much land. Most of it was ploughed and planted with a single crop which makes it only sporadically labour intensive.

The ( * ) next to the amount of time that a 30 acre villein worked on their own and their lords land serves to draw attention to the fact that the villein would have had to choose to either work less of their own land in order to fulfil the obligation or pay the lord a monetary sum in recompense for the missing day.

My aim is to explore a hypothetical *average* western European medieval village from the mid 14th century with the aim of understanding the social and economic intricacies that held these communities together. The medieval village was the basic unit of commerce in the middle ages…

a foundation stone upon which was built a network of market towns feeding goods to the rich and populous cities.

I’m interested in discovering how a few bushels of surplus produce in a tiny village can translate into exports of fine cloth and metal work destined to foreign markets.

Freeholders

The typical inhabitants of a village in the 14th century were divided into freeholders and villeins.

Both parties paid the landowner a tax for the right to live and work on the land.

With the exception of the Lord and the Clergy all freeholders on the manor are tenants and pay rent in either goods or money to the lord for the privilege of living and working there. Unlike villeins they are not bound to spend a portion of their working lives in service of the lord maintaining and farming his land.
