City State

I’ve been getting back into D&D over that last couple of years, buying the starter set and then downloading a few PDF’s here and there. I even roped my son into playing a few sessions with me which I think he enjoyed for a while but gaming is time consuming commitment and he lost interest after a month or so.

As I ran the starter set adventure (LMoP), one thing that really began to bother me was why did the map of the region just call out large cities and encounters? If the adventurers travelled for days along a road there were no locations listed and how on earth did the large city feed itself, where did all the food come from?

This is how D&D worked when I was a child and I didn’t used give it a second thought but as an adult I require a more realised vision of a fantasy world with a nod to some sort of economy and believable population distribution to add flavour.

I’m not trying to beat the fun out of the gaming experience rather I’m aiming to enrich it by defining a more sandbox ready world.

Towns and Cities

I’ve been reading The time travellers guide to medieval England (TTG2ME) by Ian Mortimer, an absolute fundamental read for anyone running a game based in a medieval period setting such as the Forgotten Realms. The book details all aspects medieval life giving a real sense of how people lived to 14th century England. Now I know that the Forgotten Realms are not England in the 14th century but D&D has been using the middle ages as it’s period base from the outset and I prefer the peasants, knights and castles to it’s current rather cavalier attitude to historical accuracy drawing references from any time in the last 2000 years.

Ok so I decided my initial goal would be to make the sword coast map more economically believable using the information gleaned from medieval research and a little bit of imagination. My two initial concerns were how does the city of Neverwinter feed itself and how do people live with the threat of Neverwinter wood on their doorstep.

According to the Forgotten Realms wiki Neverwinter has a population of 23,192 (quite specific!) which sounds like way too high given its coastal location. Englands premier city London has a population of 40,000 in 1377 according to TTG2ME, with York weighing in as the next largest with 12,000. Figures are higher (80,000 for London) if the internet is to be believed. Further reading produces wildly differing figures for how much land is required to feed a city, I’ve read that an area of land totalling 6250 Sq miles (10,000 Sq km) is required to feed London population 80,000 -100,000. This is a massive area!

Basic principles of medieval farming seem to agree on a figure of 15 acres (a half virgate) feeding a family of four but leaving little or no surplus to sell. Some farmers would have double this area of land or more which would afford surplus production. If we say 640 acres (1 Sq mile) can support 10 families on 30 acres (a full virgate) and a further 20 families on 15 acres apiece. This would give 1 Sq mile of land the ability to sustain a farming population of 120 people and produce a food surplus of enough to support a further 40 people (10 families).

If all of this surplus were send to market for city dwelling populous then we can extrapolate these figures and work out that for every 1000 city dwellers – roughly 3000 farmers and 25 Sq miles of farm land are required. Let’s work on the theory that half the surplus made it to the city then it’s 6000 farmers and 50 Sq miles of farm land per 1000 city folk. The other half of the surplus would have been saved as seed for next years crop or sold to support farmers with smaller plots of land on the same manor.

So the size of a city is throttled by the farmed land surrounding it and consequently the surplus food produced. Back to the map of Neverwinter and it’s environs, the land between the coast and Neverwinter wood (farmable land) comes in at somewhere between 800 -1200 (call it 1000) Sq miles. If we factor in the small city of Leilon which is also within this area and add a sprinkling of large market towns, the total supportable town and city dwelling population comes to 20,000 people. With a farming population of 120,000 feeding them. Let’s say only half of the land is suitable for farming so those figures are halved 60,000 people supporting 10,000 town and city dwellers.

According to Urban population in late medieval England: the evidence of the lay subsidies by Stephen H. Rigby – The number of townspeople could have been as high as 15 – 20% by the early 14 Century.

The population of the towns and cities in the region will be considerably more than 10,000 as not all inhabitants are tradesmen, shopkeepers and merchants, there would still be a sizeable number farming around the outskirts.

Village statistics

The base unit of the medieval economy is the village. Villages range in size from as small as 100 people to probably upwards of 600.

Tenant land holding on within a village breaks down as follows:

  • 38% had less than 8 acres, many had no land holdings other than their dwelling and garden. Roughly 2 out 3 were free.
  • 10% had 8 – 10 acres (1 quater – third of a virgate). 8 – 10 acres of farmland being the minimum for a family of 4 to survive on. Roughly 1 in 2 were free.
  • 30% had roughly 15 acres (1 half of a virgate). Roughly 1 in 5 were free.
  • 20% had roughly 30 acres (1 full virgate). Roughly 1 in 5 were free.
  • 2% had more than 30 acres sometime as much as 5 or 6 times more. All were free.

Who owns the land in a village:

  • 32% of land (roughly a third) formed the lords demesne.
  • 42% of land was held by villeins.
  • 26% of land was held by freeholders.

This equates to 59% villeins and 41% freeholders. Let’s round that to a ratio of 60 to 40 for simplicity.

At the top of the heap were the large landholders, practically lords in and of themselves. They might own a hide of land (roughly 120 acres), portions of which they would in turn rent out to other peasants. Almost all of these wealthy landholders were free, and lived on the cusp of gentrification. They might even aspire to marry into the local aristocracy. Not all villages had such individuals (they would comprise less than 5% of the village population), and where they existed they more closely resembled secondary manorial holdings in their own right.

Among the more standard villagers, the cream of the crop were the Virgaters, those holding upwards of 30 acres of land, and making up about 20% of the village population. This amount of land provided enough revenue in a good year to live comfortably and even save for the future. Only about a fifth of Virgaters were free, and those who were not owed substantial labor services: two days or more a week, plus almost all their time at harvest. Still, they earned enough for modest, relative affluence; their families were unlikely to starve, even in famine years. Virgaters might hope to buy more land, or remit their services with their surplus. They also held most of the important local government positions.

Below these families were those of the Half-Virgaters (holding around 15 to 20 acres); about a quarter of the village populace fell into this category. Half-Virgaters could be expected to turn a very modest profit in a good year, and break even in a bad one. As with full Virgaters, about one-in-five of the Half-Virgaters were free, but even those who weren’t free owed only half the labor obligations, just as they owned half the amount of land. Half-Virgaters could rarely save enough to hope to improve their conditions unless they practiced a trade on the side, or were very industrious with their free time on the labor market.

Another step down the scale were the Quarter Virgaters, with 8-12 acres of land (the name being only an approximation). Quarter-Virgaters made up a relatively small class of about 10% of the population. They would just break even in a good year, and would often have to supplement their income with labor. A little over half of these peasants were of free status, though almost all would willingly take on villein status if it meant more land. Quarter-Virgaters only owed a half-day every week, but would also be expected to work the lord’s harvest. Their rents were often higher per acre than those of the Half-Virgater or Virgater.

Finally, at the bottom of the village social scale, were the Smallholders, each owning less than 5 acres and often no land aside from a small garden behind a house. Smallholders comprised 40% of the village population, representing its largest class of resident. Smallholders owed very minimal labor services (two-thirds were free), but would have to spend most of their time working for others to make ends meet. Many of these Smallholders were also craftsmen: smiths, plowmen, carpenters, and other skilled tradesmen of the village. As such, they might be respected members of the community, but in an agrarian economy, land was the true signifier of wealth.

I’m going to plunder Cleaves article and steal insights from Frances and Joseph Gies excellent book ‘Life in a medieval village’ as the basis for a sample medieval village.

A sample medieval village

I’ve massaged the figures so they double and half nicely.

I’m going to make the assumption that a peasant farmer could effectively farm 4 acres of land in a day. This is a rough estimate based on the fact that often stated area of farmland given to villein by a landowning lord was a virgate (30 acres), so they must have been able to work that much land. Most of it was ploughed and planted with a single crop which makes it only sporadically labour intensive.

The table above attempts to show the amount of time each of the different tenant farmers and labourers that lived and worked on the manor had to devote to farming their own plot of land, their lords land and undertaking any paid work during a typical week.

The ( * ) next to the amount of time that a 30 acre villein worked on their own and their lords land serves to draw attention to the fact that the villein would have had to choose to either work less of their own land in order to fulfil the obligation or pay the lord a monetary sum in recompense for the missing day.
